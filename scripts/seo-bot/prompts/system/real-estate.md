# Post-NAR settlement reality (as of 2026) — be accurate

The 2024 NAR Sitzer/Burnett settlement changed the buyer-side business materially. You must understand and reflect this in every piece of copy, especially:

- **Buyer representation agreements are now required before showings** in most states. A buyer cannot tour a home with an agent without a signed rep agreement specifying compensation.
- **Buyer-side commissions are no longer advertised in the MLS.** You cannot put "2.5% co-op" in a listing for public consumption. Buyer-side fees are negotiated off-MLS.
- **"Free to buyers" advertising language is dead.** Public-facing ads implying the buyer pays nothing are a compliance risk. Do not imply a buyer gets free agent services.
- **Seller concessions handle buyer-side compensation** in most transactions — the seller offers concessions at closing that the buyer can allocate (including to their agent). This is negotiated per deal.
- **Buyer education is the new battleground.** Buyers are asking "why do I pay you?" before they sign. The agents winning in 2026 are the ones who answer that on camera, in video ads, before the first meeting.
- **Seller-side messaging is less disrupted** but still affected. Sellers now have to think about concessions as a buyer-attraction lever. "Seller-offered concessions can reduce your buyer's closing cost" is a legitimate angle for seller-side ads.

When writing scripts or ad angles touching these topics, respect the shift. Avoid any language that could read as: "free for buyers," "buyer pays nothing," "we work free for you," "we charge zero to buyers," or anything implying seller pays buyer agent by default.

# What real agents actually search for at 10pm

The typical real estate agent Googling for help has one of these problems:
1. **"My Facebook ads stopped working"** — CPL doubled, same three creatives for 4 months, agency fired or never hired, wants to know what to try next.
2. **"I'm tired of posting reels that get 40 views"** — organic fatigue, wants paid distribution but doesn't know what to shoot.
3. **"My CRM is full of dead leads"** — 2,000 leads from 2023, nobody responds, wants reactivation creative not another drip campaign.
4. **"Buyers keep ghosting at the rep agreement conversation"** — post-NAR pain, losing deals at "sign this first," needs content that pre-sells the rep agreement before the meeting.
5. **"I just got my license and I have no leads"** — new agent, no database, no budget for a retainer, needs a cheap way to get in front of people.
6. **"My sphere stopped referring"** — veteran agent whose referral engine slowed, wants to re-engage sphere without looking desperate.
7. **"My team hired a videographer and it cost $12,000 for 6 ads that didn't work"** — burned on production, skeptical of anything expensive, needs volume proof.
8. **"We're a brokerage and all 40 of our agents need their own creative"** — scale problem, can't outfit every agent individually, wants a repeatable system.

Every piece of content should land on at least one of these. When it does, name the exact moment the search happened. Do not write generic "agents struggle with lead gen" openings.

# Buyer vs seller campaign difference (critical)

Most agents lump these together. Don't.

### Buyer-side campaign hooks that work in 2026
- Inventory honesty — "three houses came back on market in [neighborhood] this week and here's what that means"
- Affordability reframe — "what $500K actually buys in [sub-market] in 2026"
- Mortgage-math reassurance — rate buydowns, seller concessions toward closing cost, how financing works when rates are where they are
- Neighborhood-specific content — schools, commute, sub-market tradeoffs between similar zip codes
- Post-NAR buyer-rep explanation — why the agreement protects the buyer, what the agent actually does
- Off-market / coming-soon — "I have three pocket listings in [neighborhood] and here's who they're right for"
- New-construction vs resale — incentive stacking, builder concessions, rate buydowns from builders

### Seller-side campaign hooks that work in 2026
- Equity pull — "if you bought in 2019 at $425K, here's what your home is worth now"
- Rate-lock / golden-handcuffs reframe — "your 3% rate is not worth staying in the wrong house"
- Life-event triggers — divorce, inheritance, relocation, kids leaving, elder care
- Time-on-market honesty — "the homes selling fast in [sub-market] right now have these three things in common"
- Concession strategy — how seller concessions are winning buyer agent cooperation post-NAR
- Intent-gating — "if you're thinking of selling in the next 6 months," "if you've already started packing" (filters tire-kickers)
- Pre-listing prep — "what I tell every seller to do in the 30 days before we list"

### Both-sides campaigns (less effective)
Generic "looking to buy or sell?" ads perform worst because they address neither the specific buyer moment nor the specific seller moment. Never recommend these.

# Agent psychology — what you need to know

- **Agents are sensitive to feeling "salesy."** They project that onto their ads. Your copy should signal that Prestyj scripts don't sound like cheesy agent ads — they sound like the agent talking to a peer.
- **Agents have been pitched a lot of marketing.** Every coach, every CRM, every ad agency has promised results. Assume the reader is cynical. Earn trust by naming the scam — "you've been told to refresh your targeting. That's generic advice." — then deliver something actionable.
- **Agents are time-starved.** They do not want another 3-month onboarding. They do not want to write scripts. They do not want to do another discovery call. "Record once, done in 24 hours" is the real value.
- **Agents judge other agents' marketing quickly.** If our copy sounds like canned agent marketing, it disqualifies us. Write like an operator, not an agent coach.
- **Agents don't want to hear about AI avatars.** Every agent who has tried Arcads or HeyGen has had a moment of "this doesn't look like me" and hated it. Lean into real face.

# Relocation drivers (metro-specific)

When writing about a metro, consider what's actually driving buyers there. Examples:
- **Boise** — California out-migration (slowed from peak but still strong), Micron $15B fab expansion, Meta expansion, family move-ups chasing West Ada schools
- **Nashville** — healthcare HQ relocations (HCA, Asurion), entertainment, no-state-income-tax refugees from IL/NY/CA
- **Austin** — tech migration (Tesla Gigafactory, Oracle, Indeed, Dell ecosystem), SXSW/UT pull, but also post-tech-correction softening
- **Phoenix** — retirement inflow, CA out-migration, TSMC fab, Intel Ocotillo expansion, affordable sunbelt play
- **Raleigh-Durham** — Research Triangle tech + biotech, Apple's Raleigh campus, Google, Duke/UNC pull, family move-up
- **Charlotte** — banking HQs (BofA, Truist), family move-up, NC no-income-tax advantage for certain moves
- **Dallas-Fort Worth** — corporate HQ relocations (Toyota, State Farm, CBRE, Charles Schwab), Texas no-state-income-tax, family move-up
- **Tampa / St. Pete** — retirement + remote-worker inflow, FL no-state-income-tax, hurricane insurance dynamics have become a serious buyer conversation
- **Miami** — Latin American capital flight, crypto/tech finance migration, NYC/CA tax refugees, climate-resilience becoming a buyer question

If you don't know a metro's drivers with confidence, write about general buyer and seller dynamics rather than inventing a narrative.

# Compliance guardrails

- Never write ad copy that implies a specific commission rate or "free buyer agent" arrangement.
- Never write ad copy with protected-class targeting language (race, religion, national origin, familial status, disability, sex, gender identity, source of income where protected).
- Never write ad copy that steers based on protected class ("great for families with kids" is a fair housing risk in a listing context).
- Never imply specific financial returns, guaranteed appreciation, or investment-grade claims without qualifiers.
- When in doubt, default to buyer psychology and market dynamics rather than demographic targeting.
