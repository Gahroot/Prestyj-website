**Subject:** Commentary: the spreadsheet error inflating AI sales-copilot ROI

Hi CIO editorial team,

Your coverage of how CIOs use AI agents to accelerate revenue growth identifies sales as an ROI-focused deployment area. I can contribute a skeptical follow-up angle: most “AI copilot vs employee” business cases compare a software subscription with a salary, then label the difference savings. That is the wrong denominator.

We use a workflow-level model instead:

1. **Human workflow cost** = loaded annual cost × share of time spent on the target workflow.
2. **AI workflow cost** = setup + 12 months of service and usage + integrations + human review.
3. **Recovered-capacity value** = qualified expert hours recovered × realized gross profit per qualified expert hour.
4. **Break-even** = recovered-capacity value + incremental gross profit − AI workflow cost.

The model often rejects low-volume deployments that look attractive in a salary comparison. It favors a hybrid design at higher volume: AI handles response, qualification, and follow-up; a human sales engineer owns discovery, solution design, and high-stakes demos.

Three points I can unpack with transparent example calculations:

- “Automated percentage” is a vanity metric when repetitive messages carry less decision value than a few technical judgments.
- Recovered capacity is not cash savings unless the business redeploys it to constrained, revenue-producing work or avoids a justified future hire.
- A credible pilot needs failure tests, a human escalation path, and gross-profit attribution—not just faster response time.
